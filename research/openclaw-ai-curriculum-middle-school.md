# OpenClaw/AI Curriculum for Middle School
## Research Notes & Full Curriculum Plan

**Prepared by:** ClawDawg  
**Date:** March 2026  
**Status:** Research Draft

---

## Executive Summary

This document presents research findings and a full modular curriculum plan for an OpenClaw/AI educational program targeting middle school students (grades 6–8, ages 11–14). The curriculum is designed to be fun, interactive, and hands-on, with students using OpenClaw (and other AI tools) directly as the primary learning instrument. The curriculum pairs with an online tutorial platform and a companion textbook.

---

## Research: Existing Programs & Best Practices

### AI4K12 Initiative (ai4k12.org)
- National guidelines for K-12 AI education (CSTA + AAAI collaboration)
- Defines **5 Big Ideas in AI** that serve as the curriculum backbone:
  1. Perception
  2. Representation & Reasoning
  3. Learning
  4. Natural Interaction
  5. Societal Impact
- Provides grade-band progressions (K-2, 3-5, 6-8, 9-12)
- Our curriculum aligns directly with these 5 Big Ideas

### MIT RAISE / Day of AI (dayofai.org)
- Free, Creative Commons curriculum developed at MIT RAISE
- Activities organized in 30-60 minute blocks for elementary, middle, high school
- Middle school units include: chatbot interactions, AI surveillance simulator, ethics discussions
- Key pedagogical approach: storytelling + ethics + interactive projects
- Teacher-friendly: designed for educators without CS backgrounds

### MIT Media Lab AI + Ethics for Middle School
- Open-source curriculum
- Teaches students to think of "algorithms as opinions"
- Students consider direct/indirect stakeholders in AI systems
- Activities: train simple classifiers, explore algorithmic bias
- Specific activity: "Exploring Word Analogies" – visualize gender/age/class biases in word embeddings
- Approach: design activities to reimagine familiar AI systems

### Code.org AI & Machine Learning Unit (CS Discoveries)
- Specifically designed for middle school CS Discoveries course
- Hands-on machine learning with tabular data
- Cross-curricular standalone lessons (brainstorming, essays, research verification)
- Students develop critical AI literacy skills
- Updated 2024-25 with GenAI modules

### Google Teachable Machine (teachablemachine.withgoogle.com)
- No-code machine learning tool
- Students train classifiers using images, sounds, or poses via webcam
- Browser-based, no installation required
- Ideal for perception and ML modules

### Machine Learning for Kids (machinelearningforkids.co.uk)
- Educational ML training tool
- Students train text, image, number, or sound recognizers
- Integrates with Scratch for creating interactive projects
- No coding required to train; Scratch blocks for building

### Prompt Engineering as Literacy
- MiddleWeb (2025): "Prompt engineering is the new literacy skill"
- Students must learn to choose best words, refine prompts, evaluate/adjust responses
- AI becomes a creative partner when prompted well
- Direct application for OpenClaw interaction modules

### OpenAI ChatGPT for Teachers
- Free education-grade workspace through June 2027 for US K-12 educators
- Privacy controls and admin features
- Model for how OpenClaw could be positioned in school environments

### Common Sense Education AI Literacy (Grades 6-12)
- "Quick, grab-and-go lessons"
- Focus on ethical, empowered AI use
- Covers AI decision-making, bias, and societal impact

### Key Pedagogical Best Practices (Harvard, research literature)
1. **Direct interaction first** – Let students experiment with AI before formal instruction
2. **Document and share** – Students record AI interactions and discuss as a class
3. **Project-based learning** – Real problems, real AI tools, real outcomes
4. **Ethics integrated throughout** – Not a standalone topic; woven into every module
5. **Collaborative design** – Students work in pairs/teams on AI projects
6. **Reflection and critique** – Students regularly evaluate AI outputs critically
7. **Multiple modalities** – Visual, auditory, kinesthetic activities for diverse learners
8. **Teacher support** – Lesson plans designed for non-CS-background educators

## Curriculum Overview

**Title:** AI Explorers: Discover, Create, and Shape Artificial Intelligence with OpenClaw

**Tagline:** "You're not just using AI — you're learning to think with it."

**Target Audience:** Grades 6–8 (ages 11–14)

**Duration:** 7 modules × ~3 weeks each = ~21 weeks (one full school year with flex time)
- Shorter version: Select 5 modules for a semester course
- Individual modules can be taught as standalone 2-3 week units

**Alignment:** AI4K12 Five Big Ideas, CSTA K-12 CS Standards, ISTE Standards for Students

**OpenClaw Integration:** Students use OpenClaw as their primary AI interaction tool throughout the curriculum — not as a passive observer but as an active participant in their learning.

## Module Breakdown

### MODULE 1: What IS AI? — Meet Your Digital Brain
**Duration:** 2-3 weeks | **Grades:** 6-8 | **AI4K12 Big Idea:** All 5 (Introduction)

**Learning Goals:**
- Define AI and distinguish AI from non-AI systems
- Identify AI in everyday life (recommendation algorithms, voice assistants, spam filters)
- Develop comfort interacting with an AI assistant (OpenClaw)
- Understand the history of AI (Turing, chess programs, modern LLMs)

**Lessons:**
1. **Lesson 1.1 – What Is AI?** (1 period)
   - Discussion: What makes something "intelligent"?
   - Sorting activity: Is it AI or not? (cards with tech examples)
   - Read: Short history of AI (Alan Turing, Deep Blue, AlphaGo, ChatGPT)
   
2. **Lesson 1.2 – AI in Your World** (1 period)
   - Homework debrief: Students documented 5 AI systems they encountered
   - "AI Bingo" game: Find examples across categories (transportation, health, entertainment, etc.)
   - Discussion: Which ones surprised you? Which would you miss most?

3. **Lesson 1.3 – First Contact with OpenClaw** (1-2 periods)
   - Tutorial walkthrough: Setting up and using OpenClaw
   - Guided exploration: Ask OpenClaw 3 different types of questions (factual, creative, opinion)
   - Lab: "OpenClaw 101" — 10-question exploration challenge
   - Reflection journal: What surprised you? What confused you?

4. **Lesson 1.4 – The Turing Test & AI Limits** (1 period)
   - Video: The Turing Test explained
   - Activity: "Is it human?" — students try to distinguish AI vs human written responses
   - Discussion: What can AI do well? Where does it fail?

**Activities:**
- 🎮 **AI Bingo** – Bingo card with AI categories; first to find real examples wins
- 🤔 **Sorting Cards** – Physical/digital cards: "AI or Not AI?" with discussion
- 📓 **OpenClaw Exploration Journal** – Students document their first AI conversations

**Lab:**
- 💻 **OpenClaw 101 Lab** – Structured 10-challenge exploration of OpenClaw capabilities

**Tutorial (Online):**
- Video: "Welcome to OpenClaw" (5 min)
- Interactive walkthrough: Your first OpenClaw conversation
- Quiz: AI or Not AI? (auto-graded)

**Assessment:**
- Reflection journal entry: 3 things I learned about AI
- Exit ticket: Name 3 AI systems and explain how each one works

### MODULE 2: How AI Sees and Hears — Perception
**Duration:** 2-3 weeks | **Grades:** 6-8 | **AI4K12 Big Idea:** Perception

**Learning Goals:**
- Understand how AI systems process sensory data (images, sound, text)
- Explore computer vision and speech recognition
- Train a simple image classifier using Teachable Machine
- Understand the difference between what a human "sees" and what a computer "processes"

**Lessons:**
1. **Lesson 2.1 – How Computers See** (1 period)
   - Unplugged activity: Pixel art — students color in grids to make images
   - Concept: How images become numbers (pixels, RGB values)
   - Demo: Zoom in on a photo until you see pixels; AI sees this matrix

2. **Lesson 2.2 – Image Recognition Deep Dive** (1 period)
   - Concept: Convolutional neural networks (visual metaphor: feature detectors)
   - Activity: Students label training images — experience what it's like to create training data
   - Demo: Draw something; AI tries to identify it (Google QuickDraw)

3. **Lesson 2.3 – Teachable Machine Lab** (2 periods)
   - Tutorial: Intro to Google Teachable Machine
   - Lab: Students train a 3-class image classifier using webcam
   - Challenge: Get highest accuracy on your test set
   - Extension: What happens when you train on only 5 images? vs 50?

4. **Lesson 2.4 – How AI Hears: Speech Recognition** (1 period)
   - Concept: Audio waveforms → spectrogram → text
   - Demo: Speech-to-text in real time
   - Activity: Train a sound classifier (clap, snap, tap) in Teachable Machine
   - Discussion: What challenges do speech systems face? (accents, noise, language)

5. **Lesson 2.5 – OpenClaw Sees & Hears** (1 period)
   - Lab: Describe an image to OpenClaw and have it respond
   - Activity: OpenClaw "20 Questions" using only descriptions (no direct names)
   - Discussion: How does text-based AI differ from vision AI?

**Activities:**
- 🎨 **Pixel Portrait** – Students draw each other in pixel art; AI tries to recognize it
- 📸 **Teachable Machine Challenge** – Most creative/accurate 3-class classifier wins
- 🎙️ **Sound Sorter** – Build a soundboard that classifies claps, snaps, taps

**Labs:**
- 💻 **Image Classifier Lab** – Teachable Machine, 3 categories, minimum 30 images per class
- 💻 **Sound Classifier Lab** – Train AI to recognize 3 different sounds

**Tutorial (Online):**
- Video: "How Computers See" (7 min animated)
- Interactive: Pixel art builder → feed to AI recognizer
- Step-by-step: Teachable Machine walkthrough
- Quiz: Perception concepts (5 questions)

**Assessment:**
- Lab report: Classifier performance, what worked/didn't, why
- Creative project: Build a "magic gesture" detector (wave = play music, thumbs up = change color)

### MODULE 3: How AI Thinks — Representation & Reasoning
**Duration:** 2-3 weeks | **Grades:** 6-8 | **AI4K12 Big Idea:** Representation & Reasoning

**Learning Goals:**
- Understand how AI represents knowledge (features, vectors, embeddings)
- Learn how AI makes decisions (decision trees, rules, logic)
- Experience building a simple rule-based system
- Use OpenClaw to explore reasoning tasks

**Lessons:**
1. **Lesson 3.1 – From Data to Knowledge** (1 period)
   - Concept: Features — what makes a cat a cat? (fur, whiskers, meow)
   - Activity: Feature extraction game — describe objects using only numbers
   - Discussion: How do you turn "furriness" into a number?

2. **Lesson 3.2 – Decision Trees: How AI Decides** (1 period)
   - Concept: If-then trees for classification
   - Unplugged activity: Build a "20 Questions" decision tree for animals
   - Demo: Interactive decision tree visualization
   - Lab: Code a simple decision tree in Scratch or block-based tool

3. **Lesson 3.3 – Pattern Recognition** (1 period)
   - Activity: Number sequence patterns; students find the rule
   - Demo: AI pattern completion with OpenClaw
   - Activity: "Broken AI" — OpenClaw given wrong patterns; why does it still try to answer?

4. **Lesson 3.4 – OpenClaw Reasoning Challenges** (1-2 periods)
   - Lab: Logic puzzles via OpenClaw
   - Challenge: Give OpenClaw a reasoning problem; verify its answer; find where it fails
   - Discussion: Is OpenClaw actually "reasoning" or something else?
   - Activity: Chain-of-thought prompting — ask OpenClaw to show its work

**Activities:**
- 🌲 **Build-a-Tree** – Teams build a decision tree for classifying school supplies
- 🧩 **Pattern Detective** – Find the rule; can OpenClaw figure it out too?
- 🤖 **"Stump the AI"** – Students find logic problems OpenClaw gets wrong; explain why

**Lab:**
- 💻 **OpenClaw Logic Lab** – 10 reasoning challenges with reflection on each

**Tutorial (Online):**
- Video: "How AI Thinks" (6 min)
- Interactive: Build your own decision tree tool
- Practice: Chain-of-thought prompting exercises
- Quiz: Representation concepts

**Assessment:**
- Design challenge: Create a "pet advisor" decision tree (at least 4 decisions, 3 outcomes)
- Reflection: One thing AI reasons well; one thing it doesn't — explain why

### MODULE 4: How AI Learns — Machine Learning
**Duration:** 3 weeks | **Grades:** 6-8 | **AI4K12 Big Idea:** Learning

**Learning Goals:**
- Understand the machine learning paradigm (data → model → prediction)
- Distinguish supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning
- Experience training and evaluating an ML model
- Understand overfitting and why diverse training data matters

**Lessons:**
1. **Lesson 4.1 – What Is Machine Learning?** (1 period)
   - Traditional programming vs ML (explicit rules vs learned patterns)
   - Analogy: Teaching a child to recognize dogs vs writing dog-recognition code
   - Activity: "Human Learning Machine" — class trains each other using only yes/no feedback

2. **Lesson 4.2 – Training Data: The Food AI Eats** (1 period)
   - Concept: Training data, labels, and how bias gets baked in
   - Activity: Students manually label 20 images of "happy" vs "not happy" faces
   - Discussion: Did everyone agree? What does disagreement mean for AI?

3. **Lesson 4.3 – Supervised Learning Lab** (2 periods)
   - Tool: Machine Learning for Kids (machinelearningforkids.co.uk)
   - Lab: Train a text sentiment classifier (positive/negative movie reviews)
   - Build a Scratch project that uses the classifier
   - Test: Does it work on reviews the AI hasn't seen?

4. **Lesson 4.4 – Unsupervised Learning: Finding Hidden Patterns** (1 period)
   - Concept: Clustering without labels
   - Activity: Students cluster a set of mystery data points by hand
   - Demo: K-means clustering visualization
   - Discussion: How might streaming services use this to make playlists?

5. **Lesson 4.5 – Overfitting and Why More Data Matters** (1 period)
   - Concept: Memorizing vs learning
   - Activity: Demonstrate overfitting by training Teachable Machine on 3 images vs 30
   - Discussion: What makes a good training dataset? (diversity, size, quality)

6. **Lesson 4.6 – OpenClaw as a Learned System** (1 period)
   - Discussion: What did OpenClaw learn from? What biases might it have?
   - Activity: Test OpenClaw on niche topics vs mainstream topics — where does it do better?
   - Reflection: What would you change about how OpenClaw was trained?

**Activities:**
- 🎮 **Human Learning Machine** – Whole-class game to simulate training feedback loops
- 🏷️ **Labeling Sprint** – Who can label 20 images fastest? Compare labels across students
- 🧪 **Overfitting Demo** – Visual experiment with Teachable Machine

**Labs:**
- 💻 **Sentiment Classifier Lab** – Machine Learning for Kids + Scratch
- 💻 **OpenClaw Bias Lab** – Test responses across different topics, demographics, time periods

**Tutorial (Online):**
- Video: "How AI Learns" (8 min with animations)
- Interactive: Drag-and-drop training data simulator
- Step-by-step: Machine Learning for Kids walkthrough
- Quiz: ML concepts (8 questions)

**Assessment:**
- Lab report: Sentiment classifier performance + 3 ways to improve it
- Short essay: "What would happen if AI was only trained on data from one city?" (ethics connection)

### MODULE 5: Talking with AI — Natural Interaction & Prompt Engineering
**Duration:** 2-3 weeks | **Grades:** 6-8 | **AI4K12 Big Idea:** Natural Interaction

**Learning Goals:**
- Understand how large language models work at a conceptual level
- Master prompt engineering as a practical literacy skill
- Learn to evaluate AI responses critically
- Use OpenClaw effectively for creative and academic tasks

**Lessons:**
1. **Lesson 5.1 – How Chatbots Work** (1 period)
   - Concept: Tokens, context windows, next-word prediction (simplified)
   - Analogy: AI as a very well-read autocomplete
   - Activity: "Predict the next word" — students play the LLM game
   - Demo: Show how adding/removing context changes OpenClaw's response

2. **Lesson 5.2 – The Art of the Prompt** (1 period)
   - Concept: Vague vs specific prompts; role-playing; few-shot examples
   - Activity: Prompt makeover — take 5 bad prompts and improve them
   - Challenge: Get OpenClaw to write a haiku about middle school lunch in exactly 3 tries (improving each time)

3. **Lesson 5.3 – Prompt Engineering Lab: The Olympics** (1-2 periods)
   - Lab: OpenClaw Prompt Olympics
     - Event 1: Best creative story opening (judge: class vote)
     - Event 2: Fastest way to explain a concept to a 5-year-old
     - Event 3: Most helpful study guide for a real upcoming test
     - Event 4: Funniest safe-for-school joke (OpenClaw writes it from your prompt)
   - Debrief: What strategies worked best?

4. **Lesson 5.4 – AI as a Study Partner** (1 period)
   - Practical skills: Using OpenClaw to explain concepts, quiz yourself, get feedback on writing
   - Activity: Students use OpenClaw to study for a quiz in another class
   - Discussion: Academic integrity — what's using AI responsibly vs. cheating?

5. **Lesson 5.5 – When AI Gets It Wrong** (1 period)
   - Concept: Hallucinations, confident wrongness, outdated information
   - Lab: "Fact Check the AI" — students verify OpenClaw claims using credible sources
   - Activity: Deliberately generate hallucinations; explain why they happen
   - Skill: How to cross-reference AI outputs

**Activities:**
- 🥇 **Prompt Olympics** – Competitive prompt engineering across 4 events
- 🔧 **Prompt Makeover** – Transform 5 weak prompts into effective ones
- 🔍 **Fact Check the AI** – Verify 10 OpenClaw claims; score a "truth report"

**Lab:**
- 💻 **OpenClaw Prompt Engineering Lab** – Structured 15-challenge progression
- 💻 **Study Partner Lab** – Use OpenClaw to study for a real upcoming test

**Tutorial (Online):**
- Video: "How LLMs Work" (7 min, animated)
- Interactive: Prompt engineering sandbox (side-by-side comparisons)
- Reference: The Prompt Engineering Cheat Sheet (PDF/download)
- Quiz: Prompt engineering scenarios (5 questions)

**Assessment:**
- Portfolio: 3 best prompts from the course + reflection on what makes them effective
- Practical: Use OpenClaw to help explain a concept from another class (with citation of sources)

### MODULE 6: Is It Fair? — AI Ethics & Societal Impact
**Duration:** 3 weeks | **Grades:** 6-8 | **AI4K12 Big Idea:** Societal Impact

**Learning Goals:**
- Identify and explain algorithmic bias and its real-world harms
- Analyze privacy implications of AI systems
- Evaluate AI applications from multiple stakeholder perspectives
- Develop informed opinions on AI policy and regulation

**Lessons:**
1. **Lesson 6.1 – Algorithms as Opinions** (1 period)
   - Concept: AI reflects the values of its creators and training data
   - Activity: MIT Media Lab "Word Analogies" — explore gender/age biases in embeddings
   - Discussion: If AI was trained mostly on internet text, what opinions did it absorb?

2. **Lesson 6.2 – Bias in the Wild** (1 period)
   - Case studies: Facial recognition bias (NIST studies), hiring algorithm bias (Amazon resume tool), predictive policing
   - Activity: "Who Gets Hurt?" — map stakeholders for each case study
   - Discussion: What would fairness look like? Can you make an unbiased algorithm?

3. **Lesson 6.3 – AI Surveillance Simulator** (1-2 periods)
   - Tool: Day of AI's Interactive Surveillance Simulator (dayofai.org)
   - Lab: Students step into roles (school administrator, student, parent, vendor)
   - Debrief: Who benefits? Who's harmed? What rights are at stake?

4. **Lesson 6.4 – Privacy and Your Data** (1 period)
   - Concept: Data collection, inference, and privacy erosion
   - Activity: "Data Trail" — trace all the data points generated in one school day
   - Discussion: What do companies/schools know about you? Is that OK?
   - Connection: What data did OpenClaw learn from? What does it "know" about you?

5. **Lesson 6.5 – Deepfakes and Misinformation** (1 period)
   - Concept: Generative AI can create fake images, audio, video
   - Activity: Real or AI? — students evaluate a set of images/text for authenticity
   - Discussion: How should society handle AI-generated misinformation?
   - Skill: How to spot AI-generated content; verification tools

6. **Lesson 6.6 – AI Debate Day** (1-2 periods)
   - Teams debate real AI policy questions:
     - "AI should be allowed in all schools"
     - "Facial recognition should be banned in public spaces"
     - "AI-generated art should be labeled"
     - "Self-driving cars should be held to a higher standard than human drivers"
   - OpenClaw research assistant: Teams can use OpenClaw to prep arguments

7. **Lesson 6.7 – What Would YOU Change?** (1 period)
   - Activity: Students redesign an AI system with fairness in mind
   - Mini-project: Create a "fairness proposal" for one AI system they use regularly
   - Reflection: What role can young people play in shaping AI?

**Activities:**
- 🧭 **Stakeholder Mapping** – Who wins, who loses from a given AI system?
- 🎭 **AI Debate** – Structured debate on real AI policy questions
- 🕵️ **Real or AI?** – Authenticity detection challenge
- 🔄 **Redesign It** – Reimagine an unfair AI system

**Labs:**
- 💻 **Day of AI Surveillance Simulator** – Role-based simulation
- 💻 **OpenClaw Bias Testing Lab** – Systematically probe for biases; document findings

**Tutorial (Online):**
- Video: "AI and Society" (8 min)
- Video: "What Is Algorithmic Bias?" (5 min, animated)
- Interactive: Stakeholder mapping tool
- Quiz: Ethics scenarios (6 questions, discussion-based)

**Assessment:**
- Fairness Proposal: 1-page written proposal to improve fairness in an AI system they use
- Debate participation rubric
- Reflection essay: "How should society decide what AI can and cannot do?"

### MODULE 7: Build with AI — Your Capstone Project
**Duration:** 3-4 weeks | **Grades:** 6-8 | **AI4K12 Big Idea:** All 5 (Applied)

**Learning Goals:**
- Apply skills from all previous modules to a real-world problem
- Design, build, and present an AI-powered project
- Practice responsible AI development principles
- Communicate AI concepts to a non-technical audience

**Project Options (student choice):**
1. **AI Problem-Solver** – Use OpenClaw + ML tools to address a real school/community problem
2. **AI-Powered Game** – Build a Scratch game with ML components (image/sound recognition)
3. **AI Ethics Documentary** – Short video exploring an AI ethics issue in their community
4. **AI Explainer** – Create a tutorial/guide teaching younger students about one AI concept
5. **AI Art Project** – Use generative AI tools to create an art installation with ethical reflection

**Lessons/Workshops:**
1. **Lesson 7.1 – Project Brainstorming** (1 period)
   - Design thinking: Empathize → Define → Ideate → Prototype → Test
   - Activity: "Problems worth solving" — students identify needs in their school/community
   - Form teams (2-3 students); choose project type; submit proposal

2. **Lesson 7.2 – Proposal & Feedback Workshop** (1 period)
   - Teams present 2-minute project pitches
   - Peer feedback using "warm/cool/wonder" framework
   - Teacher approval + scope refinement

3. **Workshop 7.3 – Build Sprint 1** (2 periods)
   - Open work time with teacher and peer support
   - Milestone: Working prototype of core feature

4. **Workshop 7.4 – Build Sprint 2 + Peer Testing** (2 periods)
   - Open work time
   - Mid-project peer testing: teams swap and test each other's projects
   - Feedback and iteration

5. **Workshop 7.5 – Build Sprint 3 + Polish** (1-2 periods)
   - Final features, documentation, and presentation prep
   - Milestone: Complete project + presentation slides/poster

6. **Lesson 7.6 – AI Expo: Project Presentations** (2 periods)
   - Science-fair style presentation to class + invited guests
   - Each team presents: problem, solution, how AI works in it, ethical considerations
   - Q&A from audience
   - Student choice awards: Most Creative, Most Impactful, Best Ethics Consideration

7. **Lesson 7.7 – Reflection & Next Steps** (1 period)
   - Individual reflection portfolio entry
   - Discussion: What would you learn next?
   - Introduction to resources for going deeper (CS, AI, ethics programs)
   - Celebration!

**Labs/Tools Used:**
- OpenClaw (conversational AI partner throughout)
- Google Teachable Machine
- Machine Learning for Kids + Scratch
- Code.org AIML unit tools
- Day of AI resources
- Student-chosen tools based on project type

**Tutorial (Online):**
- Video: "How to Pitch Your AI Project" (5 min)
- Template: Project Proposal form
- Template: Ethical Impact Assessment checklist
- Resource: Project examples from past students (gallery)

**Assessment:**
- Project rubric (functionality, creativity, ethics consideration, presentation)
- Individual reflection portfolio (covers all 7 modules)
- Peer evaluation of other projects

## Online Tutorial Platform Structure

### Platform Concept: "AI Explorers Online"
- Companion to the textbook and in-class curriculum
- Accessible on any device (tablet, Chromebook, phone)
- Gamified progression with badges and levels
- Student dashboard tracks progress; teacher dashboard shows class analytics

### Tutorial Format Per Module:
Each module has:
1. **Intro Video** (5-8 min) — Animated explainer introducing core concepts
2. **Concept Checks** — 3-5 auto-graded quiz questions after key concepts
3. **Interactive Simulations** — Browser-based activities (decision trees, data visualizers, bias explorers)
4. **OpenClaw Challenges** — Structured prompts students send to OpenClaw; reflect on responses
5. **Lab Walkthroughs** — Step-by-step video guides for hands-on tools (Teachable Machine, ML4Kids, etc.)
6. **Discussion Prompts** — Async forum questions for student peer discussion
7. **Module Badge** — Earned upon completion of all activities

### Gamification:
- **XP System** — Points for completing lessons, labs, and reflections
- **Badges** — "AI Detector," "Prompt Pro," "Ethics Advocate," "ML Engineer," "AI Builder"
- **Leaderboard** — Optional class ranking (teacher-configurable)
- **Unlockable Content** — Bonus deep-dives for students who want to go further

## Differentation & Accessibility

- **English Language Learners:** Key terms in multiple languages; visual-heavy explanations; OpenClaw can converse in students' home languages
- **Advanced learners:** Extension challenges in each module (Python implementations, deeper readings)
- **Students without home internet:** Offline activities and printable versions of all key materials
- **Students with disabilities:** All videos captioned; activities designed for multiple modalities; screen-reader compatible platform

## Implementation Recommendations

### For Schools/Districts:
1. Start with a **pilot of Modules 1 + 5** (What is AI + Prompt Engineering) — highest engagement, lowest prerequisite knowledge
2. Ensure **device access** — 1:1 Chromebook/tablet minimum for lab days
3. Provide **teacher PD** — 8-hour intro course for educators covering all 7 modules
4. Set up **OpenClaw school accounts** — age-appropriate access with appropriate privacy controls
5. Create a **student code of conduct** for AI use before starting

### Timeline Options:
- **Full-year course:** All 7 modules (September–June)
- **Semester elective:** Modules 1, 2, 4, 5, 6 (18 weeks)
- **Mini-unit:** Any single module as a standalone (2-3 week injection into another class)
- **Summer program:** Modules 1, 5, 7 (Intro + Prompt Engineering + Capstone, intensive 3-week camp)
