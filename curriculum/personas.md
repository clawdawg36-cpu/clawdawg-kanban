# Target Audience Personas
**Project:** AI Curriculum Marketing Site
**Phase:** 1 — Decisions
**Last Updated:** 2026-03-21

---

## Persona 1: Homeschool Families

**Overview:** Parent-led households where one or both parents direct all curriculum choices. Learning is flexible by design — they mix and match resources, adjust pacing to the child, and buy into curricula that fit their family's values and schedule.

### Top Pain Points
- Overwhelmed by too many options; hard to evaluate quality without trying
- Fear of wasting money on curricula kids won't finish
- Worried AI content will be inappropriate, biased, or replace critical thinking
- Need to justify curriculum choices to a spouse, co-op, or umbrella school
- No IT support — everything must "just work" with minimal setup

### Motivators
- Giving their child a competitive edge in a changing job market
- Curriculum that adapts to their child's learning style and pace
- Tangible, portfolio-ready projects kids can be proud of
- Community and support from other homeschool parents
- Affordable or one-time pricing (not another subscription)

### Language They Use
- "Charlotte Mason," "unit studies," "delight-directed," "living books"
- "We cover this at our own pace"
- "I want something my kid can actually do independently"
- "Does this align with our values?"
- "What does the output look like?"

### Words to Avoid
- "Classroom," "teacher-led," "school year," "grade level"
- "Enterprise," "district," "compliance"
- "Mandatory," "standardized," "test prep"

### Best CTA
**"See What Your Child Will Build"** — link to a sample project gallery or demo. Secondary: free trial or sample lesson download with no credit card required.

## Persona 2: Distance Education Students & Families

**Overview:** Students enrolled in accredited online schools or formal distance programs. They follow structured schedules, have assigned teachers, and need curriculum that fits inside existing LMS/gradebook workflows. Parents are involved but defer to the school's structure.

### Top Pain Points
- Screen fatigue — kids are already online all day
- Hard to stay engaged without in-person social energy
- Parents feel under-qualified to help when kids get stuck
- Worried the AI curriculum won't count toward transcript credits
- Technical issues derail learning days

### Motivators
- Structured, clear learning paths that reduce parental guesswork
- Engaging, interactive content that breaks up text-heavy courses
- Clear learning outcomes and assessments they can share with their school
- Content that complements (not conflicts with) their existing coursework
- Synchronous and asynchronous flexibility

### Language They Use
- "Our school requires…"
- "How does this integrate with [Canvas/Google Classroom/Schoology]?"
- "Will this count toward graduation requirements?"
- "We need something the teacher can track"
- "My kid is behind and needs to catch up"

### Words to Avoid
- "Replace your current curriculum"
- "Unstructured," "open-ended" (without pairing it with "scaffolded")
- "No tests," "no grades" (they need assessments)

### Best CTA
**"Download the Scope & Sequence"** — show them what the full course looks like, how it maps to learning objectives, and what assessments are built in. Secondary: LMS integration overview.

## Persona 3: Unschoolers

**Overview:** Families who reject institutional schooling entirely. Learning is child-led, interest-driven, and often project-based. They are skeptical of anything that feels like "school in a box" and highly attuned to corporate or manipulative framing. They want tools, not curriculum.

### Top Pain Points
- Most "alternative" curricula still feel coercive or adult-imposed
- Fear that AI tools will undermine genuine curiosity and self-direction
- Products marketed as "revolutionary" usually aren't
- Their kids have different interests — they don't want one-size-fits-all
- Community judgment: other unschoolers will ask hard questions about this choice

### Motivators
- Tools that empower the *child* to direct their own learning
- Honest transparency about what AI does and doesn't do
- Real projects with real-world stakes (not pretend scenarios)
- Connection to a wider community of curious, self-directed learners
- Respect for autonomy — no forced progressions, no required modules

### Language They Use
- "Self-directed," "interest-led," "autonomous learning"
- "We don't do grades or tests"
- "My kid chose this because they were curious about X"
- "We want tools, not a curriculum"
- "Does this respect the learner?"

### Words to Avoid
- "Curriculum," "course," "lesson plan," "unit," "module"
- "Age-appropriate," "grade-level"
- "Teacher," "instructor," "educator" (if used to describe adults imposing content)
- "Assessment," "quiz," "test"

### Best CTA
**"Explore the Toolkit"** — frame it as a set of tools and projects the learner can pick up when curious. No pressure, no signup wall. Let them explore freely and self-select.

## Persona 4: School Systems & Districts

**Overview:** Procurement decision-makers (curriculum directors, CTO/CIOs, assistant superintendents) evaluating tools for district-wide or school-wide rollout. Decisions are slow, involve multiple stakeholders, require vendor vetting, and must survive budget cycles and board approval.

### Top Pain Points
- Vendor lock-in and tools that disappear after a grant period
- FERPA/COPPA compliance and student data privacy
- IT infrastructure requirements and SSO integration
- Equity gaps: tools must work on Chromebooks and low-bandwidth connections
- Political backlash from parents or board members about AI in schools
- PD burden: teachers won't use what they don't understand

### Motivators
- Clear ROI tied to outcomes (test scores, engagement, graduation rates)
- Alignment with state standards (CCSS, NGSS, or state-specific)
- Turnkey professional development and onboarding
- Transparent data practices and privacy compliance
- Pilot programs that reduce risk before full commitment

### Language They Use
- "Standards-aligned," "evidence-based," "research-backed"
- "FERPA compliant," "SSO," "rostering via Clever/ClassLink"
- "Pilot cohort," "implementation timeline"
- "What does the PD package look like?"
- "We need a data processing agreement"

### Words to Avoid
- "Disruptive" (threat framing)
- "Replace teachers"
- "Just sign up and get started" (implies no procurement process)
- "No contracts" (they need contracts for compliance)

### Best CTA
**"Request a District Pilot"** — clear path to a scoped pilot with implementation support, data agreements, and outcome metrics defined upfront. Secondary: schedule a call with an implementation specialist.

## Persona 5: Individual Classroom Educators

**Overview:** K–12 teachers or community college instructors who want to bring AI literacy into their classroom but have limited prep time, no dedicated tech support, and high accountability for outcomes. They're often early adopters willing to experiment — but only if the lift is low.

### Top Pain Points
- No time to build lessons from scratch
- Worried about student misuse of AI (cheating, not learning)
- Admin may not support or may actively block AI tools
- Hard to differentiate for mixed-ability classrooms
- No budget — must find free or low-cost options or apply for mini-grants

### Motivators
- Ready-to-use lesson plans with clear learning objectives
- Activities that spark genuine student engagement and discussion
- Content that addresses AI ethics, not just AI skills
- Peer community — seeing what other teachers are doing
- Professional recognition (portfolio, publications, conference talks)

### Language They Use
- "I can use this Monday morning"
- "Does this come with a rubric?"
- "How do I explain this to my department chair?"
- "My students have varying ability levels"
- "What if a student just asks the AI to do it for them?"

### Words to Avoid
- "Homework" (many are moving away from it)
- "Replaces instruction"
- "Just give students access" (no scaffolding)
- "AI will grade for you" (undermines teacher judgment)

### Best CTA
**"Download a Free Lesson Plan"** — immediate, no-friction value. Show a sample lesson with objectives, activities, discussion prompts, and assessment rubric. No account required for the first one.

## Persona 6: School Boards

**Overview:** Elected or appointed board members who set policy, approve budgets, and represent community values. They are not curriculum experts — they rely on staff recommendations but hold final authority. They are acutely sensitive to constituent perception and political risk.

### Top Pain Points
- Fear of parent backlash over AI in schools (privacy, bias, inappropriate content)
- Budget constraints — every dollar must be justified publicly
- Liability concerns if AI generates harmful or controversial content
- Pressure to appear forward-thinking while not alienating traditionalists
- Staff are asking for tools the board doesn't understand

### Motivators
- Preparing students for a workforce that will use AI
- Positive press coverage and community pride
- Clear guardrails that prevent misuse or inappropriate content
- Credible third-party validation (university research, peer districts)
- Equity narrative: AI literacy for all students, not just affluent ones

### Language They Use
- "What are other districts doing?"
- "How does this protect our students?"
- "What's the liability exposure?"
- "Is this in the budget?"
- "What do parents think about this?"

### Words to Avoid
- "Experimental," "cutting-edge" (implies unproven risk)
- "Autonomous AI" (sounds uncontrolled)
- "Replace teachers" (political landmine)
- "Data collection" (raises privacy alarms without context)

### Best CTA
**"Download the Board Brief"** — a 2-page executive summary: what the tool does, what safeguards are in place, what peer districts have adopted it, and what outcomes were measured. No technical jargon.

## Persona 7: Public Policy Makers

**Overview:** State legislators, department of education officials, think tank researchers, and federal program officers who shape education policy, funding streams, and standards at scale. They think in systems, not classrooms.

### Top Pain Points
- AI literacy is not yet in most state standards — unclear how to mandate or support it
- Equity gaps: rural, low-income, and minority students are being left behind in AI access
- Workforce data is lagging — hard to know what skills employers actually need
- Vendor landscape is fragmented and hard to evaluate without technical expertise
- Political polarization makes AI in education a lightning rod

### Motivators
- Workforce readiness: AI literacy as economic development
- Equity and access: ensuring all students — not just those in wealthy districts — are AI-ready
- Civic AI literacy: students who understand AI as citizens, not just consumers
- Scalable, evidence-based programs that qualify for federal or state funding
- Cross-sector partnerships (industry, higher ed, K–12)

### Language They Use
- "Workforce pipeline," "21st century skills," "digital equity"
- "Evidence-based," "scalable," "systems change"
- "Underserved communities," "rural access," "broadband parity"
- "STEM pathways," "career and technical education"
- "Civic technology," "algorithmic accountability"

### Words to Avoid
- "Subscription model" (implies inequitable access)
- "Private vendor" without open-data commitments
- "Proprietary" curriculum or assessment
- "Pilot" without a scaling plan

### Best CTA
**"Read the Policy Brief"** — a research-backed document connecting AI literacy to workforce outcomes, equity metrics, and funding opportunities. Include a section on how this program qualifies for existing grant programs (E-Rate, Title IV, ESSA). Secondary: invite to a policy roundtable or briefing.

## Persona 8: Parents (General)

**Overview:** Parents who aren't homeschooling and aren't policy makers — they're just parents who heard their kid's school (or a neighbor, or a news story) mention AI curriculum and want to understand: *What is my kid actually going to learn, and is this safe?* They are protective, curious, and often skeptical of tech companies targeting children.

### Top Pain Points
- Don't understand AI well enough to evaluate what's being taught
- Worried about screen time, data privacy, and age-appropriate content
- Fear their child will learn to let AI do their thinking for them
- Concerned about bias or harmful outputs from AI tools
- Don't want their child to fall behind peers — but also don't want to be sold something

### Motivators
- Their child building something real they can show off
- Skills that will matter when their kid enters the workforce
- Transparent, honest communication about what AI can and can't do
- Proof that critical thinking and creativity are central — not replaced
- Other parents vouching for the program

### Language They Use
- "What will my kid actually learn?"
- "Is this safe for a [10/14/16]-year-old?"
- "Will this make them dependent on AI?"
- "My kid's school sent home a notice about this"
- "I want them to be prepared but I don't want them glued to a screen"

### Words to Avoid
- "Disrupting education" (sounds reckless)
- "AI-powered learning" (vague, buzzwordy)
- "Personalized algorithm" (creepy to parents)
- "Data-driven" without explaining what data and why
- "The future of education" (overused, eye-roll inducing)

### Best CTA
**"See What Your Child Will Create"** — a gallery or video of real student projects, with student voice front and center. Show outcomes, not features. Secondary: a plain-language FAQ answering the top 5 parent concerns (safety, data, screen time, critical thinking, teacher involvement).
