# Audience Research — Pain Points & Motivations by Segment

> Phase 1 Deliverable | AI Curriculum Project
> Last updated: 2026-03-21

---

## 1. Homeschool Families

### Who They Are
Parents (often one primary educator) who have opted out of traditional schooling. Range from religious conservatives to secular progressives; united by wanting control over their child's education. Frequently active in co-ops, Facebook groups, and curriculum fairs.

### Pain Points
- **Overwhelm from curriculum overload** — Too many choices, hard to compare quality
- **Credibility anxiety** — Worried their child will "fall behind" or struggle with college admissions
- **Time crunch** — One parent often doing the teaching while managing the household
- **Screen time guilt** — Conflicted about introducing more tech/AI into learning
- **Isolation** — Especially for kids with niche interests; hard to find peers at the same level

### Motivators
- Child is genuinely engaged and *excited* to learn
- Parent feels competent and confident teaching the material
- Curriculum is adaptable to their child's pace and learning style
- Real-world applicability — "What will this actually do for my kid?"
- Community validation — other homeschool parents are using and loving it
- Future-proofing — "AI literacy is a job skill my kid needs"

### Vocabulary They Use
- "Charlotte Mason," "classical," "living books," "unit study," "notebooking"
- "Rabbit trail" (following curiosity), "delight-directed learning"
- "Standards-aligned" (some want this; others actively don't)
- "Screen time," "hands-on," "narration," "self-paced"
- "My kiddo," "our homeschool," "our co-op"
- "Scope and sequence," "all-in-one," "open-and-go"

### Best CTA
**"See what a lesson looks like"** — low-friction preview (sample lesson, short demo video). Pair with a parent testimonial. Avoid gatekeeping with signups before they can evaluate.

> _"Try a free lesson — no account required."_

## 2. School Boards

### Who They Are
Elected or appointed officials overseeing K–12 districts. Balancing constituent pressure, state mandates, union contracts, and tight budgets. Actual decisions often made by curriculum directors and superintendents; board approval is the final hurdle.

### Pain Points
- **Budget scrutiny** — Every dollar spent on curriculum is a dollar not spent elsewhere; must justify ROI
- **Liability and controversy** — AI in schools is politically charged; fear of parent backlash, media coverage
- **Equity concerns** — Must demonstrate no student group is disadvantaged by new tools
- **Teacher resistance** — Curriculum that requires significant PD is a hard sell
- **State standards compliance** — Must map to adopted standards or risk funding implications
- **Vendor lock-in fear** — Bad experiences with ed-tech companies that disappear or jack up prices

### Motivators
- **Proven outcomes** — Pilot data, district case studies, measurable student results
- **Standards alignment** — ISTE, state CS frameworks, CSTA; shows due diligence
- **Low teacher burden** — "Plug and play," minimal retraining required
- **Equity story** — Bridges digital divide, works for ELL/special ed populations
- **Cost-effectiveness** — Per-seat pricing that beats alternatives; grant-eligible
- **Reputational upside** — District seen as forward-thinking and preparing students for the future

### Vocabulary They Use
- "Student outcomes," "measurable impact," "data-driven"
- "Standards-aligned," "ISTE," "CSTA," "Common Core adjacent"
- "Equity and access," "underserved populations," "ELL," "IEP"
- "Professional development," "implementation support," "teacher onboarding"
- "RFP," "procurement," "per-seat license," "site license"
- "Pilot program," "proof of concept," "stakeholder buy-in"

### Best CTA
**Request a district pilot** with outcome tracking built in. Lead with case studies from comparable districts. Offer to present to the curriculum committee.

> _"Schedule a 20-minute district briefing — we'll bring the data."_

## 3. Policy Makers

### Who They Are
State legislators, education agency officials, think tank researchers, and federal ED staff. They set frameworks, fund initiatives, and write the rules everyone else follows. Motivated by re-election/legacy, ideology, and constituent/donor pressure.

### Pain Points
- **Defining the problem** — No consensus on what "AI education" means; is it about using AI or building it?
- **Equity anxiety** — Risk of widening gaps between well-resourced and under-resourced districts
- **Speed of change** — Technology moves faster than policy cycles; fear of being behind
- **Accountability gaps** — Who's responsible when AI gets it wrong in a classroom?
- **Vendor influence** — Skepticism about tech company lobbying masquerading as education reform
- **Workforce disconnect** — Employer demands don't always map to existing graduation requirements

### Motivators
- **Workforce pipeline** — Connecting education to economic competitiveness is politically durable
- **Equity wins** — Demonstrable proof that all students (rural, low-income, minority) benefit
- **Safety and guardrails** — Curriculum that demonstrably teaches responsible/ethical AI use
- **State comparisons** — "We're falling behind [other state]" is a powerful nudge
- **Bipartisan framing** — Workforce + safety frames appeal across aisle; avoid partisan hot buttons
- **Research backing** — Academic citations, think tank endorsements, RAND/Brookings credibility signals

### Vocabulary They Use
- "AI literacy," "digital citizenship," "future-ready workforce"
- "Equity and access," "broadband divide," "Title I," "underserved"
- "Guardrails," "responsible AI," "algorithmic bias," "transparency"
- "K–12 pipeline," "STEM," "computer science for all"
- "Evidence-based," "pilot outcomes," "longitudinal data"
- "Workforce development," "21st-century skills," "economic competitiveness"

### Best CTA
**Policy brief + briefing request**. Offer a one-pager framed around workforce and equity. Invite them to an advisory council or co-design process — they want ownership, not vendor pitches.

> _"Download the policy brief — or let's talk about what your state needs."_

## 4. Unschooling Communities

### Who They Are
Families practicing self-directed education (SDE) — often inspired by John Holt, Peter Gray, or the Alliance for Self-Directed Education. Deep skepticism of formal curriculum, testing, and credentialing. Value is placed on following the child's intrinsic motivation. Often active in SDE hubs, Agile Learning Centers, and local "hacker" communities.

### Pain Points
- **Anything that feels like school** — Worksheets, grades, checkboxes, rigid sequences are automatic turn-offs
- **Loss of agency** — Curriculum that tells the child what to learn and when undermines their autonomy
- **Institutional co-option** — Fear that "AI education" is just schools trying to look modern while staying controlling
- **Fear of dependency** — Concern that AI tools replace real thinking instead of extending it
- **Community skepticism** — Hard to share something that looks like a product pitch with their crowd
- **Credentialism creep** — Anything that suggests "this prepares you for college" can feel like betrayal

### Motivators
- **Child-led exploration** — Tool responds to what *the child* wants to know, not a preset scope
- **Real-world skills** — Building something, solving a genuine problem, not simulated scenarios
- **Anti-authoritarian framing** — AI as a tool that *democratizes* access to knowledge (no gatekeepers)
- **Maker/tinkerer ethos** — Hands-on, experimental, "break it and see what happens"
- **Community endorsement** — Trust travels through peer networks, not marketing
- **Philosophical alignment** — Product that respects the child as a capable, curious person

### Vocabulary They Use
- "Self-directed," "child-led," "intrinsic motivation," "deschooling"
- "Agency," "autonomy," "consent," "living curriculum"
- "Real-world," "maker," "tinkering," "emergent learning"
- "Deschooling Society" (Illich), "Free to Learn" (Gray), "How Children Learn" (Holt)
- "Unschooling," "worldschooling," "life learning," "SDE"
- "Credentialism," "factory school," "conveyor belt"

### Best CTA
**Show, don't sell.** Share a story of a kid who used the tool to pursue their own curiosity — not to "complete a module." Invite them into co-creation. No freemium funnels, no email capture on first touch.

> _"Here's what one kid built with it — what would yours do?"_

## Cross-Segment Observations

| Segment | Core Fear | Core Hope | Trust Signal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Homeschool Families | My kid falls behind | My kid loves learning | Other homeschool parent testimonials |
| School Boards | Backlash / wasted money | District looks forward-thinking | Pilot data from comparable districts |
| Policy Makers | Inequity / accountability gaps | Workforce wins + safety cred | Research citations, bipartisan framing |
| Unschooling Communities | Loss of child's agency | Tool that follows the child | Peer stories, no-pitch community sharing |

---

## Strategic Notes

1. **No single message works across segments.** Homeschoolers and unschoolers share surface-level overlap (both outside traditional school) but have fundamentally different values. School boards and policy makers share vocabulary but operate on different timescales.

2. **The equity frame is the closest thing to a universal.** Every segment has a version of it — equity of access, equity of outcomes, equity of child agency.

3. **Lead with proof, not promises.** All four segments are skeptical of ed-tech hype. Whoever you're talking to, bring receipts first.

4. **Channel matters as much as message.** Unschoolers trust peer sharing. School boards trust district-to-district referrals. Policy makers trust think tanks and briefings. Homeschoolers trust curriculum fairs and co-op word-of-mouth.
