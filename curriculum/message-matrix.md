# Core Message Matrix — AI Curriculum

> **Purpose:** Define the primary message hierarchy for each audience segment.
> This matrix guides all copy, landing pages, outreach, and campaign decisions.

---

## How to Use This Matrix

Each audience has:
- **Core Message** — the single most important thing they need to hear
- **Supporting Messages** — 3–5 reinforcing points that build the case
- **Objection Handling** — the top concern and how to address it
- **Call to Action** — what we want them to do next
- **Tone** — the emotional register that resonates

---

## 1. Homeschool Families

**Core Message:**
> *A semester-long project your child will actually finish and be proud of.*

**Supporting Messages:**
1. Structured but flexible — fits your schedule, not the other way around
2. Built-in milestones mean your kid sees progress every week, not just at the end
3. No fluff. Real concepts, real skills, something tangible to show for it
4. Works for kids 10–18, no prior coding or math experience required
5. Your child becomes the expert in the room — they'll explain it to you

**Objection Handling:**
- *"We've started curricula before and never finished."* → This one is designed backward from completion. Every module has a deliverable. If you finish a module, you've built something real.

**Call to Action:**
Download the free starter module. Try week 1 today.

**Tone:** Warm, confident, parent-to-parent. No jargon. No oversell.

## 2. Unschool / Self-Directed Learners

**Core Message:**
> *Your kid builds a real AI. No tests. No grades. Just making.*

**Supporting Messages:**
1. This isn't a course about AI — it's a project where AI gets built
2. Learner-led: follow curiosity, skip what's boring, go deep on what isn't
3. The output is theirs — a working model they designed, trained, and can explain
4. Community of other builders — not students competing for grades
5. Adults learn alongside kids here; age doesn't determine access

**Objection Handling:**
- *"I don't want my kid sitting through another lecture series."* → There are no lectures. There's a project, tools, a community, and optional deep-dives when they want more. It's more like a workshop than a class.

**Call to Action:**
Join the builder community. Start the project whenever you're ready.

**Tone:** Anti-institutional, maker culture. Direct. Assumes the reader is smart and impatient.

## 3. Educators (K–12 Teachers)

**Core Message:**
> *Every lesson planned. Every rubric written. Just teach.*

**Supporting Messages:**
1. Full lesson plans: objectives, activities, discussion prompts, extensions
2. Pre-written rubrics for every project deliverable — adapt or use as-is
3. No need to be an AI expert. The curriculum teaches you alongside your students
4. Paced for real classrooms: 50-minute periods, homework-optional design
5. Designed to survive a sub day — clear enough for anyone to run

**Objection Handling:**
- *"I don't have time to learn a new subject to teach it."* → You don't have to. The teacher guide explains the concepts at the same level you'll need to facilitate discussion — not a PhD, just enough to lead the room.

**Call to Action:**
Download the teacher guide for Unit 1. Use it next week.

**Tone:** Collegial, respectful of time. Practical. Acknowledges that teachers are already overloaded.

## 4. School Boards & District Administrators

**Core Message:**
> *Standards-aligned, free forever, no vendor lock-in.*

**Supporting Messages:**
1. Mapped to CSTA, NGSS, and Common Core where applicable — adoption documentation included
2. Open license: copy it, modify it, host it yourself. We will never charge for it.
3. No platform dependency — runs on any device with a browser, no accounts required for students
4. Scales from one classroom to an entire district with no per-seat costs
5. Audit trail and adoption support available for rollout planning

**Objection Handling:**
- *"We've been burned by ed-tech vendors before."* → There is no vendor relationship here. The curriculum is a document and a set of tools, not a subscription. You own it the moment you download it.

**Call to Action:**
Request the district adoption packet. Schedule a 30-minute briefing.

**Tone:** Professional, concise. Addresses institutional concerns head-on. No buzzwords.

## 5. Policy Makers & Funders

**Core Message:**
> *Equity-first AI literacy at scale — before the gap widens further.*

**Supporting Messages:**
1. The AI literacy divide is forming now, along existing lines of race, income, and geography
2. This curriculum is free, open, and designed for under-resourced classrooms — not just well-funded ones
3. Reaching 1 student costs nothing. Reaching 100,000 costs the same.
4. Evaluation frameworks and outcome metrics are built in from day one
5. Early movers shape the standard — waiting means inheriting someone else's framework

**Objection Handling:**
- *"There are a lot of AI education initiatives. Why this one?"* → Most AI education teaches students *about* AI. This teaches them to *build* it. The difference in understanding — and in who ends up working on these systems — is significant.

**Call to Action:**
Review the policy brief. Connect us with your education counterpart.

**Tone:** Urgent but grounded. Data-forward. Respects that policy makers see a lot of pitches.

## 6. Parents (General / Non-Homeschool)

**Core Message:**
> *By the end, your child will have built their own AI. Here's what that means.*

**Supporting Messages:**
1. Not a coding class — a project. They make something that actually does something.
2. AI isn't going away. Getting ahead of it now, at this age, changes their options later.
3. They'll understand AI at a deeper level than most adults — including most tech professionals
4. You don't need to understand it to support them. That's the point.
5. It's free. You have nothing to lose by trying it.

**Objection Handling:**
- *"My kid isn't a 'tech person'."* → Good. This isn't for tech kids — it's for curious kids. The students who do best aren't the ones who already love computers; they're the ones who want to understand how things work.

**Call to Action:**
Show your kid the intro video. Let them decide if they're interested.

**Tone:** Honest, low-pressure. Speaks to the parent's anxiety about the future without catastrophizing. Treats parents as intelligent adults who don't need hype.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

These messages apply across all segments and should be woven into all communications:

| Theme | One-liner |
|-------|-----------|
| **Completion** | Designed to be finished, not abandoned |
| **Ownership** | Students build something they own and can explain |
| **Openness** | Free, open license, no lock-in, ever |
| **Equity** | Built for under-resourced contexts, not retrofitted for them |
| **Real skills** | Not literacy about AI — literacy *through* building AI |

---

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **Don't say "cutting-edge" or "future-ready"** — vague and overused
- **Don't lead with the technology** — lead with the outcome for the person reading
- **Don't use images of kids on laptops looking engaged** — it's every other ed-tech campaign
- **Don't promise job outcomes for kids** — too far, too soon, undermines trust
- **Don't position against other curricula** — frame around what we are, not what others aren't
